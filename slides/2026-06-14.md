<!--
This is the one I find most interesting to think about, so bear with me for a second.

We've been saying "a familiar is different from a chatbot" for a while. But saying it and having a spec for it are very different things. The Familiar Contract is the spec.

Five properties: named identity, defined purpose, bounded authority, persistent memory, and human belonging. Every familiar has to satisfy all five. If something doesn't, it's not a familiar — it's a bot with a costume.

What I find genuinely exciting about this is the word "auditable." The contract isn't just a document. It's machine-readable schemas with a validator. That means you can check whether a familiar actually satisfies the contract — not just claim it does.

Think about what that unlocks: you could run the validator before a deployment. You could diff two versions of a familiar's identity and see what changed. You could build tooling that refuses to launch a familiar that doesn't pass contract validation. Continuity becomes an engineering property, not just a design intention.

This is v0.1.0, which means we want pushback. If you read the spec and think something's missing or wrong — please open an issue. The contract only gets stronger if people poke at it.
-->

<div class="url-chip">github.com/OpenCoven/coven · v0.0.39</div>

<!--
Let's talk about the runtime — the thing underneath everything else.

Coven core is on the v0.0.39 line. This week's notable work: daemon lifecycle hardening, pre-trust cwd handling for interactive Claude sessions, and a deliberate architectural decision about workflow execution that I want to explain.

There's been a question on the table about whether to adopt Conductor as the workflow substrate. The decision this week was: not yet. And the reasoning is important — it's not "Conductor is bad," it's "we should earn the right to add that complexity by first making our own execution model solid."

The key invariant is this: step N's output should be committed to the event log before step N+1 starts. Full stop. That one constraint is what makes workflows resumable, replayable, and debuggable. It's what lets Cave show you a timeline of what happened and let you step back through it.

That's also what makes Higgsfield — image and video generation — a runtime-portable Coven skill rather than a one-off integration. Any skill that respects the event log can be composed into a durable workflow. That's the design.

The foundation first. Then the framework.
-->

<!--
I want to talk about something that doesn't always get its own slide, but deserves one.

As familiars get more capable — as they can draft posts, send messages, work your repo, surface tasks — the question of how much to trust them becomes more acute. And the temptation in that situation is to just... not add friction. Ship fast, trust the model, see what happens.

We're not doing that.

Gitleaks pre-commit hooks are now across OpenCoven repos. The Cave Comms concept is explicit: familiars prepare posts, but approval stays in the human's hands before anything goes out. Logs are visible. Boundaries are named. Users stay in control.

Here's the thing I believe about this: the approval step is not friction. It is the trust contract. Every time a familiar checks in before acting, it's demonstrating that it knows its boundaries. And every time that check-in goes smoothly — the human approves it, the thing goes out, it's good — that's trust being built in both directions.

The path to a familiar you can actually delegate to isn't "skip the approval." It's "have so many smooth approvals that you start to feel confident handing more over." That's how you earn autonomy. Not by claiming it. By building the track record.
-->
