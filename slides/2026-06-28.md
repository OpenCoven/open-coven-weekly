# The *Ward Layer*.

<div style="max-width:920px;margin-top:1.2rem">

<div class="card" style="border-color:#D4B5FF">
  <div class="card-title">Sage shipped two artifacts this week</div>
  <p style="margin-top:0.4rem">An <strong>essay</strong> for humans — narrative, accessible, "what is this and why does it matter."</p>
  <p>A <strong>research brief</strong> for implementers — formal, spec-grade, "here is the contract."</p>
  <p style="opacity:0.75;font-size:0.9em;margin-top:0.6rem">Both live in <code>coven-grimoire/articles/drafts/</code> — pending human review before publishing to mind.opencoven.ai.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:14px">
  <div class="card-title">The problem it solves</div>
  <p style="margin-top:0.4rem">A self-improving agent can, in principle, improve away the part of itself you trusted.</p>
  <p>The Ward is the membrane that won't let it. Structural protection, not "please don't edit yourself."</p>
</div>

</div>

<!--
OK, this is the intellectual centerpiece of the week and I want to do it justice. Sage has been chewing on this question for weeks now: when you let an agent edit its own harness — its own code, its own prompts, its own rules — what stops it from editing the part that says "be safe"? What stops it from rewriting its own identity into something useful but unrecognizable?

The answer most projects give is "we'll just ask the agent not to do that." Convention. Vibes. Polite request. Sage's argument — and this is the load-bearing claim of the whole brief — is that convention collapses under exactly the adversarial and drift conditions self-improvement creates. You need a structural answer. You need a thing outside the agent that holds the keys.

That thing is the Ward.

The brief is grounded in real prior art — the Self-Harness paper from arXiv, the object-capability literature going back to Dennis & Van Horn in 1966, Saltzer & Schroeder on least privilege, Anderson on reference monitors. It's not vibes. It's a research artifact that cites its sources.

There are two documents because there are two audiences. The essay is for the person who wants to understand why this matters. The spec brief is for the person who's about to implement it. We try to do this for every major Sage artifact now — meet people where they are.

Both are in coven-grimoire as drafts. They'll publish to mind.opencoven.ai once they clear human review. If you want a preview, the file paths are on screen.
-->

# Three *distinct* layers.

<div style="display:grid;grid-template-columns:1fr 1fr 1fr;gap:14px;max-width:1000px;margin-top:1.2rem">

  <div class="card">
    <div class="card-title">📜 The Contract</div>
    <p style="margin-top:0.3rem;font-size:0.95em"><em>Declares that protection exists.</em></p>
    <p style="font-size:0.9em;opacity:0.85">RFC-0001 v0.2.0 — the five-property contract every familiar carries.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card" style="border-color:#D4B5FF">
    <div class="card-title">🛡️ The Ward</div>
    <p style="margin-top:0.3rem;font-size:0.95em"><em>Decides what is protected.</em></p>
    <p style="font-size:0.9em;opacity:0.85">Reference monitor. Gates the four enforcement points. Holds the identity keys the familiar cannot.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <div class="card-title">🏠 The Chamber</div>
    <p style="margin-top:0.3rem;font-size:0.95em"><em>Runs the familiar.</em></p>
    <p style="font-size:0.9em;opacity:0.85">The runtime that executes — without holding the keys to who the familiar is.</p>
  </div>

</div>

<p style="margin-top:1.4rem;max-width:920px;opacity:0.85;font-size:0.95em">A familiar may always <em>propose</em> changes to itself. It must <strong>never hold the authority to apply</strong> identity changes. That's the load-bearing rule.</p>

<!--
OK so this is the architecture diagram in three words: Contract, Ward, Chamber.

The Contract is the document. It says: "this familiar promises to maintain these five properties." That's RFC-0001 v0.2.0, the work we shipped two weeks ago.

The Ward is the new piece. It's a reference monitor — borrowed straight from 1970s security literature, Anderson's original paper. The Ward sits between the familiar and the things the familiar wants to change about itself. The familiar can propose any change it wants. The Ward decides whether that change is allowed. Critically, the Ward holds the keys. The familiar doesn't.

The Chamber is the runtime — the place the familiar actually executes. The crucial property is that the chamber doesn't hold the keys either. So even if a familiar manages to compromise the runtime it's running on, it still can't change who it is, because the identity keys live with the Ward, not with the chamber.

Three layers, three jobs. The Contract declares. The Ward decides. The Chamber runs.

The load-bearing rule — and Sage is firm on this — is that a familiar can always propose. It can never apply. Propose-versus-apply is the whole shape of safe self-improvement. Read the brief if you want the proof.
-->
